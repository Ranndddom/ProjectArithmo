1.1 Place Value and Number Sense

Place Value

Imagine a number like 1,234,567,890. Each digit holds a specific "place" that represents a power of ten. Think of it like this, moving from right to left, each spot is ten times bigger:

Reading and Writing Numbers

Standard Form: This is the way we typically write numbers, using digits. For example: 1,234,567,890.

Word Form: This is how we say the number aloud, using words. For example: One billion, two hundred thirty-four million, five hundred sixty-seven thousand, eight hundred ninety.

To convert between standard and word form, you need to be very familiar with the place values. We group the digits into threes, separated by commas, to make it easier to read: (Billions), (Millions), (Thousands), (Ones).


Examples


Standard Form:98,765
Word Form:Ninety-eight thousand, seven hundred sixty-five

Standard Form:4,003,210
Word Form:Four million, three thousand, two hundred ten

Standard Form:123,456,789
Word Form:Ninety-eight thousand, seven hundred sixty-five

Standard Form:123,456,789
Word Form:One hundred twenty-three million, four hundred fifty-six thousand, seven hundred eighty-nine

Standard Form:5,678
Word Form:Five thousand, six hundred seventy-eight

Standard Form:900,000,000
Word Form:Nine hundred million

Comparing and Ordering Whole Numbers

Comparing means determining the relative size or value of two or more numbers or mathematical entities. It's a fundamental process that helps us understand the relationships between different quantities.

How to Compare?

1. Number of Digits: The number with more digits is always greater. For example, 1234 is greater than 123

2. Comparing Digits from Left to Right:

– If the numbers have the same number of digits, compare the digits starting from the leftmost (largest place value) position.
– If the digits in that place are the same, move to the next digit to the right and compare.
– Continue this process until you find digits that are different. The number with the larger digit in that place is the larger number.

Symbols In Comparing

Greater than: This symbol (>) shows that one value is larger than another. For example, 7 > 3 means that 7 is greater than 3.

Less than: This symbol (<) shows that one value is smaller than another. For example, 2 < 6 means that 2 is less than 6.

Equal to: This symbol (=) shows that two values are the same. For example, 5 = 5 means that 5 is equal to 5.

Examples


1. 7 > 3: Seven is greater than three.
2. 1 < 5: One is less than five.
3. 10 = 10: Ten is equal to ten.
4. 25 > 12: Twenty-five is greater than twelve.
5. 8 < 11: Eight is less than eleven.

Ordering To order a set of numbers, compare them pairwise as described above and arrange them from smallest to largest or largest to smallest as needed.

Examples


Numbers 3, 8, 1, 5, 11
Ordered 1, 3, 5, 8, 11

Numbers 15, 2, 7, 10, 4, 1
Ordered 1, 2, 4, 7, 10, 15

Numbers 25, 12, 30, 9, 18
Ordered 9, 12, 18, 25, 30

Numbers 100, 50, 25, 75, 10
Ordered 10, 25, 50, 75, 100

Numbers 1, 1, 5, 2, 1, 8
Ordered 1, 1, 1, 2, 5, 8

6.1 Problem Solving Strategies

Problem Solving Strategies

Problem-solving strategies are systematic approaches or techniques used to find solutions to complex issues or challenges. They provide a structured framework for analyzing problems, generating potential solutions, and selecting the most effective course of action. Here's a breakdown of what they encompass:

Step-by-Step Process for Solving Word Problems

1. Understand the Problem:
- Read the problem carefully, multiple times.
- Identify the question being asked.
- Determine what information is given.

2. Plan Your Approach:
- Identify the key information and eliminate unnecessary details.
- Determine what mathematical operations are needed.
- Decide if you need to use any formulas.
- Draw a picture.
- Write an equation.

3.Solve the Problem:
- Carry out the necessary calculations
- Show your work clearly and neatly.

4. Check Your Answer:
- your calculations for accuracy.
- Ask yourself if your answer makes sense in the context of the problem.
- Substitute your answer back into the original problem to see if it fits.
- Make sure you have the correct units.

5.State Your Answer:
- Write your answer in a complete sentence.
- Include the appropriate units.

Example


Problem: "A farmer has 15 apples and 23 oranges. How many fruits does he have in total?"


1. Understand

- Question: How many fruits in total?
- Given: 15 apples, 23 oranges.


2. Plan:

- Operation: Addition.
- Equation: 15 + 23 = ?


3. Solve:

- 15 + 23 = 38


4. Check:

- 38 is a reasonable total.
- Double check the addition.


5. State:

- The farmer has 38 fruits in total.

Tips for Tackling Word Problems

1. Read Actively, Not Passively: Don't just scan the words. Engage with them. Imagine the scenario.

2. Highlight Key Words: Look for words that indicate mathematical operations:

– "Total," "sum," "in all" (addition)

- "Difference," "less than," "remain" (subtraction)

- "Product," "times," "of" (multiplication)

- "Quotient," "divided by," "shared equally" (division)


3. Draw a Picture or Diagram: Visual aids can make abstract problems concrete.

4. Estimate Before Calculating: This helps you check if your final answer is reasonable.

5. Don't Fear the Unknown: If you're stuck, try writing down what you do know. Sometimes, this sparks an idea.

6. Work Backwards: If you know the final result, you might be able to figure out the steps that led to it.

7. Use Real-World Connections: Relate the problem to situations you've encountered in your own life.

2.1 Understanding Fraction

Fraction

fraction represents a part of a whole or a part of a set. It's a way to express a quantity that is less than one whole unit. Fractions are written in the form a/b, where 'a' is the numerator and 'b' is the denominator.

Numerator (a): The numerator represents the number of parts we are considering or selecting. It tells us "how many" of the equal parts we have.

Denominator (b): The denominator represents the total number of equal parts into which the whole is divided or the total number of items in the set. It tells us "how many" equal parts the whole is divided into. The denominator cannot be zero.


Parts of a Whole: This refers to dividing a single object or quantity into equal parts.

Example

A circular pizza is cut into 8 equal slices. If you take 3 slices, you have 3/8 of the pizza. The numerator (3) represents the slices you took, and the denominator (8) represents the total number of slices.



Parts of a Set:This refers to a group of distinct objects or items.

Example

A basket contains 7 fruits. 3 are apples, and 4 are oranges. The fraction of apples in the basket is 3/7. The 3 represents the number of apples, and the 7 represents the total number of fruits.

Identifying Equivalent Fractions

Equivalent fractionsare fractions that represent the same value or proportion, even though they may have different numerators and denominators. They represent the same point on a number line.

Finding Equivalent Fractions: The fundamental principle is that multiplying or dividing both the numerator and the denominator of a fraction by the same non-zero number creates an equivalent fraction.

Example

The fraction 1/2 is equivalent to 2/4. Multiply both the numerator (1) and the denominator (2) of 1/2 by 2: (12)/(22) = 2/4.

Comparing And Ordering Fractions

Comparing fractions involves determining which fraction is greater than, less than, or equal to another fraction. Ordering fractions means arranging a set of fractions from least to greatest or greatest to least.

Comparing Fraction

Same Denominator: When fractions have the same denominator, the fraction with the larger numerator is the larger fraction.

Same Numerator:When fractions have the same numerator, the fraction with the smaller denominator is the larger fraction. This is because the whole is divided into fewer pieces, so each piece is larger.

Common Denominator:Find a common multiple of the denominators (least common multiple is best). Convert each fraction to an equivalent fraction with this common denominator. Then compare the numerators.

Ordering Fraction

Ordering Fractions: Use the comparison methods above to compare pairs of fractions and then arrange them in the desired order.

Example

Order 1/2, 2/3, and 3/4. Using a common denominator of 12: 6/12, 8/12, 9/12. So the order is 1/2, 2/3, ¾.
